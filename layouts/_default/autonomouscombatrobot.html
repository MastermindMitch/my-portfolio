What:A combat robot that uses sensors to find its position and the enemy’s position for faster attacks and defensive maneuvers.

How:A simulation was built to test ideas and code faster. Many routes were investigated and attempted, such as using AI or SLAM. Due to extreme limitations, the only real-time solution was to hard-code a vision system that took advantage of the environment.

Results:The simulation proved everything I was looking for and we could reliably track our position. I built a mock-up of the arena to start testing a real-life version. The real bot showed some promising aspects and areas needing work, but time ran out as college ended.

Perseverance:This project is my magnum opus. I had people tell me it was too hard, too long, even impossible. Nobody believed in it, so I built everything myself — CAD, code, and research. Even when my club refused funding, I persisted. This project symbolizes my determination to push boundaries and lead innovation.

Image Processing

Below describes how we take the video feed from our robot to locate our position and the enemy’s.

Top-down view of the simulation

What our bot sees

Final estimate of our position in the arena

First, we blur the image to remove small details and noise, focusing on the main walls and shapes.

Convert to HSV color space to make color-based filtering more robust to lighting differences.

Apply a binary threshold: wall-colored pixels become white (1), everything else black (0).

Calculate the average wall height (red line). Anything intersecting that line but not a wall is an enemy (blue box).

Keep only the floor region, count pixels vertically until hitting the enemy, then convert to distance.

Remove walls and floor, isolate the enemy, and measure its bounding box whenever visible.

Apply Canny Edge Detection to outline the top and bottom of each wall.

Measure wall thickness by counting vertical pixels between upper and lower edges.

Use Hough Line Transform to extract wall equations. Two vertical lines indicate a post; we mark their midpoint with a white line.

Convert pixel measurements to distances. Turquoise lines = averaged walls; red dots = corners; orange lines = enemy distance and angle.

Rotate all data to align with the robot’s heading for accurate coordinate mapping.

Place the bot in the arena, align to the wall, and mark potential locations (yellow). The closest to the last known position is chosen.
